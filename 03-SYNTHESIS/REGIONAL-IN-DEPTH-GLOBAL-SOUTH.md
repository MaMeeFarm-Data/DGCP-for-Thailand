# Regional In-Depth Analysis: Global South  
## Transition, Informality, and Visibility Stress in Data Governance

**Written:** December 2025  
**Scope:** Regional Data Governance & Real-World Data (RWD)  
**Approach:** Structural / Comparative / Non-Prescriptive  
**License:** MMFARM-POL-2025  

---

## 1. Purpose of This Regional Analysis

This document examines **structural data governance conditions**
commonly observed across regions broadly referred to as the Global South.

It does not define a single geography, rank countries,
or propose policy solutions.

The purpose is to identify **recurring structural patterns**
related to transition, informality, and visibility
that affect real-world data across diverse contexts.

---

## 2. The Global South as a Structural Condition

Rather than a fixed location, the Global South represents
a **structural condition** characterized by:

- Rapid economic and social transition
- High levels of informal and semi-formal activity
- Mixed institutional capacity
- Uneven digital infrastructure
- Strong dependence on daily human labor

These conditions shape how data is generated, recorded, and observed.

---

## 3. Informality as a Core Structural Feature

Across many Global South contexts:

- Large portions of economic activity occur outside formal registries
- Daily labor generates value without formal data traces
- Institutional datasets underrepresent lived reality

Informality is not an anomaly.
It is a **persistent structural feature**.

---

## 4. Visibility Stress and Data Gaps

A recurring challenge is **visibility stress**:

- Lived conditions change faster than data systems update
- Aggregated indicators smooth over local disruption
- Crisis signals emerge at the ground level before recognition

This creates a widening gap between
what is experienced and what is recorded.

---

## 5. Platform Expansion Without Data Sovereignty

Many Global South regions experience:

- Rapid adoption of digital platforms
- Asymmetric data ownership favoring platforms
- Limited access to platform-generated insights for public understanding

Digital participation increases activity visibility
without increasing data sovereignty.

---

## 6. Feedback Loops Under Transition

Under transition conditions:

- Feedback loops are fragmented and delayed
- Policy responses rely on partial observability
- Resilience depends heavily on informal adaptation

This reinforces the importance of understanding
how ground-level data could complement institutional systems.

---

## 7. Structural Relevance of DGCP™

DGCP™ is not presented as a solution for the Global South.

However, the Global South context highlights why
**parallel, non-institutional observability layers**
are conceptually relevant:

- They can coexist with informality
- They emphasize continuity over registration
- They foreground human-origin data

These characteristics align with conditions of transition.

---

## 8. Comparative Insight for Thailand

Thailand shares several Global South structural traits:

- Informal and semi-formal economic activity
- Platform dependence without ownership
- Rapid socio-economic change
- Limited continuous ground-level proof

This comparison situates Thailand within a broader
structural landscape without labeling or prescription.

---

## 9. Conclusion

The Global South illustrates that data governance challenges
often arise not from lack of technology,
but from **structural distance between institutions and lived reality**.

Understanding this condition strengthens comparative research
and supports inquiry into complementary data layers
that preserve ground-level truth without disrupting institutions.
